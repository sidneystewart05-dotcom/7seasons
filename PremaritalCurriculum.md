## Prerequisite: Faith Agreement

Before any session begins, the couple must affirm agreement with these four foundations. Everything that follows flows from this agreement.

1. **Salvation** — Jesus Christ is Lord and Savior. Apart from Him there is no salvation.
2. **Scripture** — Every word of the Bible is true, from God, used to encourage, instruct, rebuke, and refine believers.
3. **Covenant** — Marriage is a covenant designed, guided, approved, and blessed by God. He is the defining third strand in every Christ-centered marriage.
4. **Permanence** — Per Mark 10:6-9, the only biblical way to exit the marriage covenant is through death. Divorce is not the goal; this entire curriculum is aimed at preventing it.

> *Note: This curriculum is explicitly Christ-centered. It is not forced on anyone, but biblical principles are the structural foundation for all content and advice.*

## Session 2 — Money: What's the Big Deal?

**Core concept:** The way you view money will always determine how you live your life. Money is not the goal — it's a tool. When it becomes the goal, it destroys. Every couple must build a shared philosophy before they build a shared budget.

**Universal principle:** You are a steward of resources, not an owner. That single mindset shift changes everything — how you spend, how you give, how you save, and how you fight about it.

**Objectives:**
- Understand each individual's financial propensity (spend / save / budget / earn)
- Establish a shared money philosophy: combined accounts, unified identity
- Build a practical financial framework the couple can use immediately
- Understand how financial habits will interact across the seasons of marriage

**Core position:**
- "What's yours is yours and what's mine is mine" → that's a single lifestyle. Stay single.
- Unity means everything is combined. There is no "my account" and "your account."
- Money problems are the leading cause of marital conflict and divorce. This conversation cannot wait.

---

### The 5 Financial Disciplines

**1. Work Diligently**
Consistent, disciplined effort is the foundation of financial health. Don't put all your energy in one place — sow broadly, because you don't know which effort will produce. Laziness and financial health cannot coexist.

*Universal principle: Effort is the seed. Results are the harvest. Plant every day.*

**2. Give First**
Generosity belongs at the top of the budget — not what's left over at the bottom. Couples who give proactively (before they feel like they can afford to) develop a fundamentally different relationship with money than couples who give reactively. Giving first is a posture, not a math problem.

*Universal principle: Generosity is a discipline, not a feeling. Build it into the budget first.*

**3. Save and Invest**
- **Save:** The wise build reserves. The fool consumes everything they have. Savings is not optional — it is protection.
- **Diversify:** Spread investments across multiple ventures. You don't know which will succeed, so don't concentrate all your risk in one place.
- **Invest broadly:** Don't limit yourself to what's familiar or local. Think expansively.

*Universal principle: What you store protects you. What you diversify protects you further.*

**4. Manage Your Mind About Money**
Anxiety about the economy, the market, or uncertainty will paralyze decision-making. Overthinking leads to inaction. Waiting for the perfect moment to save, invest, or act is itself a financial failure.

At the same time: peace of mind is worth more than maximum returns. Don't sacrifice contentment chasing accumulation.

*Universal principle: Don't let fear stop you from planting. Don't let greed stop you from resting.*

**5. Enjoy What You Have**
Building financial health as a couple should include intentional enjoyment — not just accumulation. Wealth without the capacity to enjoy it is a tragedy. Budget for life, not just survival.

*Universal principle: Money is supposed to serve your life. Not the other way around.*

---

### The Net Worth Framework

> **Net Worth = Income − Expenses − Debt**

**Categorize every expense on two axes:**

| | **Regular** (predictable, consistent) | **Irregular** (variable or occasional) |
|---|---|---|
| **Significant** (necessary to life/wellbeing) | Mortgage/Rent, Insurance, Core giving | Gas, Electricity, Medical |
| **Insignificant** (not necessary) | Gym membership, Subscriptions, Streaming | Eating out, Impulse purchases, Entertainment |

**The exercise:**
1. Each person lists every expense they currently have
2. Categorize each one into the 2×2 matrix
3. Together, decide which Insignificant expenses survive the marriage budget
4. This reveals financial personality faster than any questionnaire

---

### Individual Financial Discovery Questions

Before building a shared budget, explore each person's financial history and tendencies:
- Are you a natural spender or saver — and where did that come from?
- What did money look like in your home growing up? Was it talked about openly or avoided?
- What's your relationship with debt? Do you avoid it, use it strategically, or not think about it?
- How do you feel about giving — as a habit, not a one-time decision?
- What does financial security feel like to you? What number, or what circumstance, makes you feel safe?
- What's the most financially irresponsible thing you've ever done? (This one matters.)

## Session 3 — Big Decisions (MOC Board)

**Source:** *Fate of the Seed* (FOTS), Greenhouse Lesson 2

**Core concept:** MOC = Multitude of Counselors. Every person needs a trusted, curated board of advisors across the key areas of their life — and every couple must build one together.

> "Whoever has your ear, has your future." — Pastor Matthew Reneau

**Objectives:**
- Establish each person's individual MOC Board
- Build a shared MOC Board for the couple
- Establish the SMIC decision-making method for tough choices
- Answer: What happens when we disagree?

### The MOC Board Framework

**Structure:**
- Break your life into **4 categories** (spiritual, financial, relational, professional — the diagram shows 4 quadrants with God at the center)
- Assign **3 people per category** (up to 12 counselors total)
- God is at the center of the board — He is the primary counsel

**Qualifications for each counselor:**
1. Readily accessible
2. Has experience in that area
3. Has your best interest at heart

**Why it works:**
> "This will extend your capacity to gain knowledge and allow for a more efficient decision-making process."
- Proverbs 15:22 — Plans fail for lack of counsel, but with many advisers they succeed
- Proverbs 11:14 — In an abundance of counselors there is safety
- Proverbs 24:6 — In abundance of counselors there is victory
- Psalms 1:1 — Blessed is the man who walks not in the counsel of the wicked

### The SMIC Decision Method

When facing a major decision, walk through:
- **S** — Situation: Describe the situation clearly
- **M** — MOC: Consult the relevant counselors from your board
- **I** — Impact: What is the potential impact of each option?
- **C** — Conclusion: Reach a conclusion based on counsel + prayer

**This is how the couple navigates disagreement** — not by fighting until someone wins, but by pausing to consult the MOC, weigh the impact together, and arrive at a conclusion neither made alone.

### Related Content from FOTS
The *Fate of the Seed* curriculum also covers **Structure & Order** in this same lesson — relevant to how a marriage should be organized:
- "Structure is powerful, order is beautiful"
- Most marriage problems stem from either the absence of structure, or disorder within a structure
- Biblical structure model: Husband → Wife → Children (Ephesians 5:21–6:4)
- Scripture: 1 Corinthians 14:33 — God is not a God of disorder, but of peace

**Sternberg's 3 Elements of Love** (also covered in this session — relevant to Session 1 and Session 6):
- **Intimacy** — connectedness, closeness
- **Passion** — physical attraction, sex
- **Commitment** — decision to be in relationship and maintain it
- All three together = Love. Missing one or two leads to: liking, infatuation, empty love, romantic love, companionate love, or fatuous (pointless) love

## Session 5 — Communication

**Core concept:** Arguing right is a sign of love — it says "even at my worst, I still respect you." The goal is never to win. The goal is to be understood, and to understand.

**Universal principle:** Words build or destroy. How you argue reveals what you actually think of your partner. The SNAP method keeps conflict from becoming contempt.

---

### Opening Exercise — The Good and The Bad

Each person answers separately before discussing together.

| The Good | The Bad |
|---|---|
| What's the nicest thing your partner ever said to/about you? | When was the last time you got into an argument? |
| Did they know how much it meant to you? Why/why not? | What was it about? How did it play out? |
| What's the nicest thing you've ever said to/about them? (I love you doesn't count) | How did your partner feel? How did YOU feel after? |

### Using Words to Build Up
- Speak to your partner with loving, specific words — not generic affirmation
- Effective communication is not always about WHAT is said, but HOW it is said
- Non-verbal communication is always speaking — learn to read it
- *Fate of the Seed* principle: Spend a lot of time in "Determine if it will be fruitful" before speaking. Once you say it, there is no going back.

---

### The SNAP Method — Arguing Right

SNAP is the framework for how to fight without destroying each other.

| Letter | Principle | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| **S** — Seek the Root | Don't argue the surface. Find the real issue. | "We're not fighting about dishes. What need isn't being met right now?" |
| **N** — Never Punch Low | Attack behaviors, never character. | "What you did hurt me" vs. "You are selfish." The first is fixable. The second is a wound. |
| **A** — Accept Blame | Own your part — even when it's small. | "I raised my voice. I brought up the past. That wasn't fair." Non-negotiable. |
| **P** — Present a Solution | Don't end the argument — resolve it. | Agree on something specific before you walk away. Not "we'll do better." Specific. |

### Counselor Tips (to preserve in AI voice):

> "Some couples sweep conflict under the rug. 'It's just not worth it.' But what you avoid doesn't disappear — it accumulates. Avoidance is a slow form of erosion. These conversations are worth having precisely because you love each other."

> "In the heat of an argument, you may both be talking past each other — so in your own head about the issue that you miss what your partner is actually saying. The goal is not to respond. The goal is to understand."

---

### The Argument Experience — SNAP in Practice

This is not just a teaching concept. It becomes a live tool couples use during actual conflict.

**How it works:**
Each spouse enters the experience separately — they never see each other's responses until the AI has processed both.

**Step 1 — S: Seek the Root**
Seven guides each person past the presenting issue toward what's underneath.
> *"What do you think this is really about — not the surface issue, but the deeper need that isn't being met?"*

The AI distinguishes surface arguments (dishes, money, time) from root arguments (feeling unheard, disrespected, taken for granted, unsafe).

**Step 2 — N: Never Punch Low**
As each person describes the conflict, the AI monitors the language being used.

- Character attack: *"He's always so selfish"* → AI redirects: *"Let's focus on what he did rather than who he is. What specifically happened that felt hurtful?"*
- Behavior description: *"She interrupted me three times and walked away"* → AI continues

This step coaches the principle in real time — it's not a correction, it's a redirect.

**Step 3 — A: Accept Blame**
> *"Is there any part of how this unfolded that belongs to you — even something small? What might you have done differently?"*

This step is non-optional. The AI does not let it be skipped. The depth and honesty of each person's answer here is itself significant data.

**Step 4 — P: Present a Solution**
> *"What would resolution look like to you specifically? Not 'I want things to be better' — what would actually need to happen, or what agreement would need to be reached?"*

---

### Fault Weighting — When One Person Is More Wrong

The SNAP experience does not pretend all conflict is mutually caused. Sometimes fault is weighted clearly on one side. The AI reflects this honestly.

**How it surfaces:**
The synthesis after both accounts are submitted will reflect asymmetry when it exists — particularly in the A step. If one person owns significant responsibility and the other's ownership is minimal or empty, that gap is real information and is named.

> *"Looking at both accounts together, the behavior that appears to have caused the most harm in this conflict sits primarily on [Person B's] side — specifically [the action described]. [Person A] has demonstrated significant ownership of their role. A productive next step would be for [Person B] to go deeper on Step A before resolution is attempted."*

**The rule:** The AI names behavior — never character. It renders observations, not verdicts. *What you did* is addressable. *Who you are* is an attack.

**Three situations where the AI does not adjudicate — it flags for the counselor:**
1. **Patterns of repeated wrongdoing** — control behaviors, persistent lying, manipulation. This is not a communication problem. It requires a human.
2. **Any mention of abuse** — physical, emotional, sexual. The AI immediately steps out of the arbiter role, acknowledges what was shared, and routes to crisis resources and counselor review.
3. **Irreconcilable factual disputes** — when accounts are so contradictory the AI cannot responsibly assess what happened. The gap itself is flagged as something worth exploring in session.

---

### Counselor View vs. Couple View

**What the couple sees:** A warm, honest synthesis of both perspectives — where they overlap, where they differ, and a suggested path toward resolution.

**What the counselor sees:** The unfiltered assessment — ownership imbalances, language patterns, behavioral flags, recurring patterns against their relationship profile, and anything worth raising in the next session. The counselor report does not soften what the couple view does.

## Session 7 — Expectations

**Core concept:** Expectations are the silent killer of marriages. The path to happiness is releasing your expectations of your spouse and doubling down on your expectations of yourself.

**The Happiness Equation:**
> **Happiness = Results − Expectations**

**The Exercise:**
1. Each person writes an exhaustive list of everything they expect from their spouse
2. Each person writes an exhaustive list of everything they expect from themselves
3. Each person reads their self-expectations list aloud
4. Each person discards (literally throws out) their spouse-expectations list
5. The message: Marriage flourishes when you focus entirely on being the best version of yourself — regardless of how the other person behaves

**The Biblical Parallel:**
> The Jews missed Jesus because of misplaced expectations. They expected a warrior-king who would elevate Israel politically. He didn't come like that. Their expectations blinded them to who He actually was. Whatever you expect your spouse to be — if you hold onto those expectations, you'll miss who they actually are.

**Core principle:**
You cannot control your spouse. You can only control yourself. Give everything you have without expecting anything in return. That is the key to marriage.

## Notes for App Integration

- **Faith-forward, not faith-agnostic:** This curriculum requires explicit faith agreement as a prerequisite. The existing 7 Seasons discovery engine is designed as "faith agnostic" — the premarital track will be distinctly Christ-centered. These are two different products within the same app.
- **Counselor voice matters:** Sid's tone is warm, direct, occasionally blunt, and deeply pastoral. The AI prompts for this track need to reflect that — not generic relationship advice.
- **Individual + joint sessions:** Some exercises are individual (expectations lists, financial propensity) and some are joint (communication exercises, role discussions). The app needs to support both formats.
- **The MOC Board** may need its own feature — a way for couples to identify and record their counselors, possibly with contact info and a defined role.
